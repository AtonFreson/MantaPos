*   **Cons:** Overkill if the track tilt can be pre-measured or assumed constant. Calculating Jacobians adds computational overhead (though minimal for 2D).
    

### 3\. The Error-State Kalman Filter (ESKF)

The ESKF is typically the go-to algorithm for integrating IMU data. Instead of estimating the nominal state (position, velocity, orientation) directly, it estimates the _error_ in the state.

*   **Why it fits:** It handles the high-frequency integration of the IMU in the background and only uses the Kalman Filter to correct the accumulated drift when a slower sensor (like Aruco or pressure) arrives.
    
*   **Pros:** Highly robust to IMU noise and the absolute best way to handle 3D rotations (quaternions).
    
*   **Cons:** Unnecessary complexity for your specific setup. The primary benefit of an ESKF is avoiding quaternion singularities and handling complex rotational dynamics. ### The 3 Advanced Options

#### 3\. Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF)

Instead of forcing the non-linear equations into a linear box using calculus (Jacobians), the UKF embraces the non-linearity. It uses the "Unscented Transform" to deterministically select a small set of sample points called "sigma points". It passes these exact points through your actual non-linear IMU kinematic equations and then reconstructs the mean and covariance of the new state.

*   **Pros:** It captures second and third-order non-linearities, making it inherently more accurate than the EKF. It completely eliminates the need to derive and code complex Jacobian matrices.
    
*   **Cons:** It requires more processing power than an EKF, but since you are running this on a desktop, this is irrelevant.
    

#### 4\. Particle Filter (PF) / Sequential Monte Carlo

The Particle Filter throws out the assumption that your system's uncertainty is a neat, symmetric Gaussian bell curve. It scatters thousands of "particles" (hypothetical states) and propagates all of them forward. When an ArUco or Pressure measurement arrives, it weights the particles based on how closely they match the measurement, killing off the bad ones and multiplying the good ones.

*   **Pros:** It is the ultimate brute-force method for highly non-linear systems and non-Gaussian noise. Because your ArUco rotation data is "erroneous due to math errors," the noise profile might be heavily skewed. The PF can handle that gracefully.
    
*   **Cons:** It is notoriously difficult to tune. You often encounter "particle deprivation" (where all particles collapse into a single wrong hypothesis). It requires immense computation, and extracting a smooth, continuous trajectory from a cloud of particles can be messy.
    

#### 5\. Invariant Extended Kalman Filter (InEKF)

This is the modern state-of-the-art for robot pose estimation. It formulates the robot's state not as a standard vector, but as a matrix on a Lie Group—specifically  $SE\left(2\right)$  for your 2D planar movement with 1D rotation. It leverages invariant observer theory to ensure that the error dynamics of the system are mathematically state-independent.

*   **Pros:** It solves the fundamental flaw of the standard EKF. Because the Jacobians in an InEKF do not depend on your current flawed state estimate, the filter does not suffer from false overconfidence. It converges incredibly fast and tracks IMU integration beautifully.
    
*   **Cons:** The math is heavily theoretical. Formulating your state and measurements as Lie Group matrices requires a very firm grasp of advanced linear algebra.

This is exactly where the rubber meets the road in real-world sensor fusion. What you are describing are classic **Extrinsic Calibration** errors. The physical sensors are mounted slightly crooked relative to each other and the track, and the camera’s intrinsic parameters might have slight distortions.

The short answer is: **Yes, the Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF) can absolutely account for this.** It does this through a technique called **State Augmentation** (also known as Simultaneous State and Parameter Estimation). Because you are using a non-linear filter like the UKF, you don't just estimate your moving robot; you make the filter estimate the crookedness of the sensors on the fly.

Here is how the filter handles each of your specific points:

### 1\. The ArUco X-Variation (The "Yaw/Pitch Leak")

If your track perfectly limits movement to the Y-Z plane, but your ArUco camera reports a  $\pm 3$  cm shift in X as the unit moves left and right along Y, your camera is likely rotated slightly relative to the rail (Yaw). As the unit moves sideways, it physically moves closer to or further from the camera's focal plane, causing Y-axis movement to "leak" into the X-axis measurement.

*   **The Filter's Solution:** We add an alignment angle, let's call it  $\psi _{cam}$  (Camera Yaw), to the filter's state vector. The filter knows the true movement is in Y and Z. During the UKF measurement update, it takes its internal Y estimate, rotates it by  $\psi _{cam}$ , and predicts what the faulty ArUco X and Y values _should_ be. By comparing this prediction to the actual ArUco data over time, it naturally converges on the true value of  $\psi _{cam}$  and corrects the X-drift.
    

### 2\. IMU and Camera Misalignments

You have three distinct coordinate frames: the Global frame (defined by the rails and gravity), the IMU frame, and the Camera frame. None of them perfectly align.

*   **The Filter's Solution:** We handle this by defining static Rotation Matrices in the filter's math. We augment the state vector with the Euler angles representing the misalignment between the IMU and the Global frame ( $\theta _{imu_roll},\theta _{imu_pitch},\theta _{imu_yaw}$ ).
    
*   When the UKF integrates the IMU accelerations, it first multiplies them by this estimated rotation matrix to project them into the true Global Y-Z plane.
    
*   Because the UKF uses "Sigma Points" instead of manually derived Jacobians, passing your data through these trigonometric rotation matrices is computationally painless.
    

### 3\. Pressure is Strictly Z (Your Anchor)

This is your saving grace. Because fluid pressure is dictated by gravity, it is perfectly aligned with the true global Z-axis.

*   **Why this matters:** In Kalman filtering, this is called an "observable anchor." If your IMU is tilted, the filter might struggle to tell if the unit is accelerating or if gravity is just leaking into the Y-axis. The pressure sensor cuts through that ambiguity. It provides an absolute, un-tiltable reference for Z, which forces the filter to correctly calculate the IMU's pitch and roll offsets to make the math balance out.

Here are the mathematical equations for the **Asynchronous Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF)**, structured specifically for your multi-rate, 10-state architecture.

Because your data arrives asynchronously, the time step  $\Delta t$  is not fixed. Every time _any_ sensor data packet is popped from your sorted dataset, you calculate  $\Delta t=t_{current}−t_{previous}$ , run the **Prediction Step** to catch the state up to the current time, and then run the **Update Step** using the specific sensor data that just arrived.

### 1\. The State Vector & Weights Setup

Let  $L=10$  be the number of states in your vector:

$$
x=\left[y​z​v_{y}​v_{z}​\varphi _{imu}​\theta _{imu}​\psi _{imu}​\varphi _{cam}​\theta _{cam}​\psi _{cam}​\right]^{T}
$$

The UKF uses deterministic "Sigma Points" to capture the mean and covariance. You need  $2L+1=21$  sigma points.

First, calculate the weights for the mean ( $W_{im}$ ) and covariance ( $W_{ic}$ ). You choose scaling parameters  $\alpha$  (typically  $10^{−3}$ ),  $\beta$  (typically 2 for Gaussian distributions), and  $\kappa$  (typically 0).

$$
\lambda =\alpha ^{2}\left(L+\kappa \right)−L
$$

$$
W_{0m}=\frac{\lambda }{L+\lambda }
$$

$$
W_{0c}=\frac{\lambda }{L+\lambda }+\left(1−\alpha ^{2}+\beta \right)
$$

$$
W_{im}=W_{ic}=\frac{1}{2\left(L+\lambda \right)}for i=1,...,2L
$$

* * *

### 2\. Generating Sigma Points

Before a prediction or update, generate the 21 sigma points based on your current state estimate  $x^_{k−1}$  and covariance matrix  $P_{k−1}$ . Let  $\gamma =\sqrt{L+\lambda }$ . We use the Cholesky decomposition of the covariance matrix:

$$
X_{0}=x^_{k−1}
$$

$$
X_{i}=x^_{k−1}+\gamma \left(\sqrt{P_{k−1}}\right)_{i}for i=1,...,L
$$

$$
X_{i+L}=x^_{k−1}−\gamma \left(\sqrt{P_{k−1}}\right)_{i}for i=1,...,L
$$

_(Where  $\left(\sqrt{P_{k−1}}\right)_{i}$  is the  $i$ \-th column of the lower triangular matrix)._

### 3\. The Prediction Step (Time Update)

When a timestamp triggers, you push all 21 sigma points through your non-linear kinematic physics model  $f\left(X,\Delta t\right)$ .

**The Kinematic Model  $f\left(X,\Delta t\right)$ :** For each sigma point, the angles remain constant, but the velocity and position update using the IMU accelerations ( $a_{x},a_{y},a_{z}$ ) rotated by the specific IMU angles contained _within that specific sigma point_.

Let  $R_{imu}$  be the 3D rotation matrix formed by  $\varphi _{imu},\theta _{imu},\psi _{imu}$ . The true global acceleration is:

$$
\left​a_{global_x}a_{global_y}a_{global_z}​\right​=R_{imu}\left​a_{x}a_{y}a_{z}​\right​−\left​009.81​\right​
$$

Since physical movement is constrained to the Y-Z plane, we ignore  $a_{global_x}$  for integration (though it’s used to penalize yaw in the update step). The new dynamic states for each sigma point become:

$$
v_{y,k}=v_{y,k−1}+a_{global_y}\Delta t
$$

$$
v_{z,k}=v_{z,k−1}+a_{global_z}\Delta t
$$

$$
y_{k}=y_{k−1}+v_{y,k−1}\Delta t+\frac{1}{2}a_{global_y}\Delta t^{2}
$$

$$
